Grammar Guide

The Four Conditionals

Each conditional has a precise purpose — from universal laws to imagined pasts. Study the structure, absorb the meaning, see it in action.

00 Zero Conditional

General Truths & Facts

If + Simple Present
→
Simple Present

Expresses universal facts and scientific laws — situations that are always true. The result follows automatically, with no exceptions.

If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.

If plants don't get sunlight, they die.

💡 Both clauses use the same tense — Simple Present.
01 First Conditional

Real Future Possibilities

If + Simple Present
→
will + Base Verb

Used for real and likely future events. The speaker believes the condition could genuinely happen and predicts a result.

If it rains tomorrow, I will stay home.

If she studies hard, she will pass the exam.

💡 Never use will in the if-clause of a First Conditional.
02 Second Conditional

Hypothetical Present

If + Simple Past
→
would + Base Verb

Describes imaginary or impossible present situations. The speaker knows the condition isn't real — it's a wish or a fantasy.

If I had a million dollars, I would travel the world.

If she were taller, she would become a model.

💡 Use "were" for all subjects in formal English (If I were…).
03 Third Conditional

Imagined Past Events

If + Past Perfect
→
would have + Past Participle

Talks about past situations that didn't happen and their imaginary results. Commonly used to express regret or criticism.

If I had studied harder, I would have passed.

If they had left earlier, they would have caught the train.

💡 The Third Conditional always refers to the past — it's too late to change it.

Quick Reference

Second Unreal present Simple Past would + base verb Hypothesis
Third Imagined past Past Perfect would have + pp Regret
